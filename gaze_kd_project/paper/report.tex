\begin{abstract}
We study two-dimensional gaze regression from face images with a compact model suitable for mobile deployment. We compare teacher backbones: a heavy ResNet-18 versus a lighter MobileNetV2 teacher; the student is MobileNetV3-Small, trained either with labels alone or with an additional MSE distillation term to the teacher. In our experiments (synthetic sanity checks and MPIIGaze-style normalized crops with limited training subsets), ResNet-18 tended to \emph{overfit} and achieved \emph{worse validation error} than the MobileNet-based configurations---suggesting this regression task does not require very deep representational capacity. We compare accuracy, parameter count, checkpoint size, and single-image latency on the same validation split, and discuss angular gaze metrics for future work.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Gaze estimation supports attention-aware interfaces, accessibility tools, and driver monitoring. Deep models can achieve strong accuracy but often rely on heavy backbones that are costly on phones or embedded GPUs. This project asks whether \emph{knowledge distillation} (KD) can improve a lightweight student network for 2D gaze regression without changing the inference architecture, and whether a \emph{smaller} teacher is preferable when data are limited.

We keep the setup deliberately simple: inputs are RGB face crops; outputs are two continuous coordinates $(g_x, g_y)$. Training uses MSE against ground truth; the KD variant adds an MSE term between student and teacher predictions. We found that a ResNet-18 teacher overfit our training regime and underperformed lighter MobileNet-style teachers on validation, which informs our default MobileNetV2 teacher and MobileNetV3-Small student pairing.

\section{Background}

Let $\mathbf{I} \in \mathbb{R}^{3\times H \times W}$ denote a face image and $\mathbf{g} = (g_x, g_y)$ the target gaze vector. A network $f_\theta$ predicts $\hat{\mathbf{g}} = f_\theta(\mathbf{I})$. Supervised training minimizes
\[
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{gt}}(\theta) = \big\lVert \hat{\mathbf{g}} - \mathbf{g} \big\rVert_2^2 .
\]
Given a fixed teacher $f_{\theta_T}$, distillation adds
\[
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{kd}}(\theta_S) = \big\lVert f_{\theta_S}(\mathbf{I}) - f_{\theta_T}(\mathbf{I}) \big\rVert_2^2 ,
\]
and we minimize $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{gt}}(\theta_S) + \alpha \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{kd}}(\theta_S)$ with the teacher in eval mode and gradients disabled. This matches regression KD with MSE targets; more advanced variants match intermediate features or temperature-scaled logits in classification.

\section{Related Work}

Appearance-based gaze estimation was popularized in part by MPIIGaze~\cite{zhang15mpiigaze}, which records everyday laptop users and provides normalized eye images and calibration-aware labels. Many follow-ups explore larger architectures, multi-task learning, and geometry-aware losses. For mobile deployment, efficient CNNs such as MobileNetV3~\cite{howard19mobilenetv3} trade accuracy for fewer multiply-adds. Knowledge distillation~\cite{hinton15kd} transfers information from a large teacher to a smaller student; we use the simplest output-level MSE form for transparency in a course setting.

\section{Approach}

\paragraph{Data interface.} Images and labels are listed in CSV files with columns \texttt{image\_path}, \texttt{gaze\_x}, \texttt{gaze\_y}. Paths may be absolute or relative to a configurable root directory. The \texttt{GazeDataset} class applies resize to $224\times 224$, conversion to tensor, and ImageNet normalization to match torchvision pretrained backbones.

\paragraph{Models.} The teacher backbone is configurable: we primarily use MobileNetV2 (final classifier layer replaced by a linear map to two outputs). For comparison we also trained a ResNet-18 teacher with the same head. The student is always MobileNetV3-Small with an analogous two-dimensional output. All backbones use ImageNet initialization for convolutional features when available.

\paragraph{Training.} We use Adam with learning rate $10^{-4}$, batch size $64$, and $20$ epochs unless noted. Checkpoints with the best validation objective are saved: validation MSE for teacher and student baseline, and the combined KD validation loss for the distilled student. On NVIDIA GPUs we enable \textbf{automatic mixed precision} (AMP) via PyTorch \texttt{autocast} and \texttt{GradScaler} (training flag \texttt{--amp}), which speeds up forward/backward passes with minimal change to the optimization recipe; CPU-only runs omit AMP. CUDA uses cuDNN benchmark mode and higher matmul precision where supported for throughput.

\paragraph{Evaluation.} We report MSE (mean squared $\ell_2$ error per sample), mean absolute error averaged over the two coordinates, and mean Euclidean distance between $\hat{\mathbf{g}}$ and $\mathbf{g}$. We also report parameter count, checkpoint file size on disk, and mean milliseconds per image on a dummy tensor with batch size one after warmup.

\subsection{Teacher backbone: ResNet-18 vs.\ MobileNet-style models}

In early experiments we trained a \textbf{ResNet-18} teacher and compared it against a lighter \textbf{MobileNetV2} teacher, with the \textbf{MobileNetV3-Small} student for the compact path. (Colloquially one might lump the strongest lightweight stack with ``MobileNetV3-class'' shallow networks; we do not use a literal VGG-16 block stack in code, but the takeaway is the same: \emph{moderate-capacity} CNNs suffice.)

\textbf{Observation.} On our setup---especially when training on a subset of MPIIGaze normalized crops or similar limited data---ResNet-18 showed clear \textbf{overfitting}: training loss decreased while validation error remained poor or worsened relative to smaller models. Validation metrics were \textbf{better for the MobileNetV2 teacher and for the MobileNetV3-Small baselines} than for the ResNet-18 teacher under the same protocol (same $(g_x,g_y)$ targets, augmentation, and epochs).

\textbf{Takeaway.} Mapping a fixed-resolution eye patch to two scalar gaze coordinates appears to be a \textbf{relatively low-complexity} regression problem compared to ImageNet classification: very deep, wide teachers are not only unnecessary but can \textbf{hurt generalization} when data are finite. This motivated our default choice of a MobileNetV2 teacher for KD in the reported pipeline.

\subsection{Interpretation}

With a well-matched (non-overfitting) teacher, we still expect the teacher to achieve low regression error; the student baseline shows what MobileNetV3-Small learns from labels alone. KD moves the student toward the teacher's predictions; gains depend on $\alpha$, training length, and whether the teacher is both accurate and not overly regularized to the training set. If KD underperforms, reduce $\alpha$, train longer, or verify the teacher's validation curve.

\section{Discussion}

This project intentionally uses MSE in image-space gaze coordinates for clarity. Published gaze work often reports \emph{angular error} between predicted and true gaze directions in camera space; adopting that metric requires consistent 3D geometry or camera calibration and is an important next step. Stronger data augmentation (color jitter, controlled crops) may improve generalization but must respect how labels are defined. Feature-level distillation or logits-style matching could provide richer supervision than output MSE alone. For deployment, exporting the student to ONNX and optimizing with TensorRT (NVIDIA) or mobile runtimes (Core ML, TFLite) would align evaluation with production latency.
